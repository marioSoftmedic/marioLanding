# Why the price of a medical exam is a data problem (not a customer service one)

A patient messages the lab via WhatsApp.

They send a blurry photo of a handwritten medical order. They ask: "How much is this with Fonasa tier C?".

On the other side, a customer service executive has to zoom into the photo, decipher the doctor's handwriting, open three different tabs in their LIS (Laboratory Information System), search for the codes, cross-reference the values with the institutional tariff, and add them up with a calculator.

Seven minutes later, they reply to the patient.

The patient had already messaged two other labs and booked an appointment with the one that answered first.

In most B2B meetings I have with lab directors, they tell me their problem is "customer service" or "staff taking too long".

They are attacking the symptom. The real problem is a data problem.

## The medical order as unstructured intent

We believe a medical order is a legal document. To the healthcare system, yes. To the patient, it is just unstructured purchasing intent.

The mistake labs make is forcing humans to act as text "parsers" and business rule engines at the same time.

When we launched Shuri (Examya's clinical agent for WhatsApp), the bet wasn't "putting a bot to chat with the patient". The bet was taking that unstructured intent (the photo) and converting it into a deterministic transaction in milliseconds.

## Decoupling the process

For the patient to get a fast and error-free quote, we built the workflow based on data architecture, not "chat" architecture:

**1. Specialized Medical OCR:** Extracting text from the image is not enough. The agent must normalize Chilean medical *slang* (knowing that "lipid prof." is a Lipid Profile and not something else).

**2. Semantic Matching (pgvector):** Instead of exact text searches in the LIS, we use embeddings. The agent searches the extracted medical concept against our FONASA tariff database and the lab's own pricing list.

**3. Deterministic Pricing Engine:** An LLM cannot do math or make up prices. If the model hallucinates a price, the legal risk is huge. That's why the agent only extracts codes (e.g. `0302047`). The final price is calculated by a hardcoded engine (TypeScript) that applies the exact rules per health insurance and patient tier.

## The "Total Automation" gotcha

Here is the trap in B2B: if you go to a lab and tell them "the bot will quote and schedule everything on its own", you scare them.

In healthcare, precision matters more than absolute speed.

That is why in Examya, the `ShuriStateMachine` architecture incorporates the `WAITING_HUMAN_APPROVAL` state.

The agent does 90% of the heavy lifting (OCR, FONASA code matching, price calculation) in less than 2 seconds and prepares the budget draft. The human executive just looks at the screen, confirms the exams match the photo, and clicks "Approve".

The patient receives their quote in 10 seconds, without errors, and the human never loses control.

## The Zoom Out

In digital health, the patient experience doesn't improve by putting more executives to answer messages or installing generic chatbots that don't understand medicine.

It improves when we treat daily interactions as what they are: data capture, structuring, and flow problems.

When a lab understands that WhatsApp is not "a customer service channel" but their patient's "main clinical data capture interface", the conversation changes entirely.
