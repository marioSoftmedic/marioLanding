# Adapter Layer: Connect Your LIS to FHIR Without Breaking Production

When a clinical lab or clinic faces interoperability regulations, the IT team's first reaction is usually panic.

They assume that to interoperate under the HL7 FHIR standard they must migrate their old LIS (Laboratory Information System), change databases, or rewrite the core software that manages daily operations.

That is the fastest way to freeze your budget and put operational continuity at risk.

In digital health, there is a much safer and cheaper alternative: building an Adapter Layer.

---

## The Danger of Direct Coupling

Many vendors try to solve interoperability by directly modifying the legacy LIS database to make it FHIR-aware.

This approach creates three major issues:

1. **Operational Risk:** Any change to the LIS schema can break billing, sample intake, or analyzer communication.
2. **Lack of Flexibility:** If local health ministry or authority requirements change, you have to modify the production core software all over again.
3. **Vendor Lock-in:** You depend entirely on the LIS vendor to update their system, which usually takes months and costs thousands of dollars.

Interoperability does not require changing your databases. It requires translating their language through a controlled, auditable layer.

---

## The Adapter Layer Architecture

An Adapter Layer is an independent software layer that acts as a translator between the legacy world (SQL, flat files, proprietary APIs) and the FHIR world.

It operates in three simple steps:

### 1. Asynchronous Ingestion
The LIS notifies events using database triggers, message queues, or log tailing. The adapter receives this data without blocking the laboratory's main workflow.

### 2. Mapping and Translation
The adapter translates relational tables into FHIR resources. For instance, it transforms a row in the `Patients` table into a `Patient` resource, and result records into a set of `Observation` resources linked to a `DiagnosticReport`.

### 3. Validation and Dispatch
The adapter validates that the JSON matches local profiles (such as Core-CL in Chile) and delivers it to the central FHIR repository or the receiver.

---

## How to Design Your First Pilot in 4 Steps

Do not try to map your entire database model on day one. Start with a focused workflow.

### Step 1: Isolate the Orders and Results Flow
Focus exclusively on the lifecycle of a common test, such as a blood count or blood glucose test.

### Step 2: Define the Synthetic Data Contract
Test the translation using fictitious data. This allows you to validate FHIR resources without worrying about patient privacy or security in the initial phases.

### Step 3: Implement Schema Translation
Map the LIS relational database structure to the corresponding FHIR resources:

```text
LIS: Orders_Table -> FHIR: ServiceRequest
LIS: Samples_Table -> FHIR: Specimen
LIS: Results_Table -> FHIR: Observation
LIS: Reports_Table -> FHIR: DiagnosticReport
```

### Step 4: Automate Conformity Testing
Use official FHIR validators to ensure the adapter output complies with local interoperability rules.

---

## What We Learned With Fhirex

When designing Fhirex at Examya, we decided to use this adapter approach.

We found that isolating translation from the LIS reduces integration risk, because it allows the workflow to be tested with synthetic data before touching real data or critical processes.

Furthermore, if the LIS database structure changes, we only need to update the mapper in the adapter, leaving the main clinical workflow completely untouched.

The true value of digital health interoperability is not in the complexity of the architecture, but in the ability to connect systems without disrupting the daily routine of healthcare professionals.
