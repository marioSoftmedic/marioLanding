## The mirage of brute force

The natural instinct when you first discover multi-agent orchestration is brute force. You have a massive problem, so you launch five parallel agents to write code or generate content, thinking they'll finish in a fifth of the time.

I did that too. The result was always the same: race conditions, overwritten files, cross-hallucinations, and a _git diff_ that was terrifying to look at.

## What nobody tells you about parallel generation

What nobody tells you about LLM orchestration is that parallel generation is extremely fragile. Agents lack implicit context of what the agent next door is doing. If two _workers_ try to refactor the same service at once, they will destroy each other's work.

## The Scout-Worker-Reviewer pattern (by Alan Buscaglia)

At Examya and within my Cotocha orchestrator, I stopped trying to parallelize writing entirely. Instead, I decided to adopt the brilliant pattern designed by **Alan Buscaglia**: the **Scout-Worker-Reviewer**.

Here is how we adapted it to our daily workflow: when there's a complex task, the orchestrator never delegates "execution" directly. First, it launches a **Scout** sub-agent (`sdd-explore`). Its only rule is that it is strictly forbidden from editing files. Its job is to read the codebase, map the blast radius, and propose a technical plan (a _spec_).

Once the plan exists, a single **Worker** enters (`sdd-apply`). This is the only agent with write permissions, but its context is strictly bounded to executing the plan the Scout made. It doesn't invent, it doesn't improvise.

## The secret: Isolating responsibilities

And here is the secret: when the Worker finishes, the **Reviewers** step in. *Here* is where I use massive parallelism. I launch independent, adversarial reviewers (`review-risk`, `review-readability`, and a blind _Judgment Day_ protocol). Unlike writing, parallel reading is cheap, fast, and collision-free.

```bash
# The real flow in OpenClaw
1. Orchestrator -> launches Scout (read-only)
2. Scout -> returns Spec + Tasks
3. Orchestrator -> launches 1 Worker (write)
4. Worker -> returns Diff
5. Orchestrator -> launches N Reviewers in parallel
6. Reviewers -> force refactor or approve
```

Token costs go up slightly, but the success rate (code that compiles on the first try and doesn't break tests) skyrockets.

Automation isn't about throwing models to type blindly at speed. It's about isolating responsibilities. If you want your AI agents to stop breaking production, take the keyboard away from 80% of them and put them to read instead.

## The next step

The next step in my backlog is integrating a predictive _guard_ that evaluates if a task is small enough to skip the Scout entirely and go straight to the Worker, saving latency on mechanical edits.
