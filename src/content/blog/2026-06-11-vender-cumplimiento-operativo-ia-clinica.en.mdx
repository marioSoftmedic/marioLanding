"Why does your implementation cost 80 UF when there are bots for $20 a month?"

That’s the question that separates toys from infrastructure tools. Today, while preparing a strategic meeting for a **leading regional clinic**, the answer hit me: in B2B healthcare, you don't sell optimization; you sell **legal peace of mind**.

If you try to sell to a Head of Digital Projects based only on "the bot replies fast," you'll lose on price. But if you talk about **Law 21.719** and how a data handling error can cost up to 20,000 UTM in fines, the conversation changes radically.

### The 'Bot-as-a-Service' Mistake

The market is flooded with solutions promising to automate a clinic's WhatsApp in 5 minutes. The problem is these solutions rarely understand **operative compliance**.

Building a bot that "says hello" is easy. Building a system that guarantees informed consent traceability, manages ARCO-P rights, and complies with Law 21.668 interoperability standards is another league entirely. That’s where the ticket price goes up, and for good reason.

### The Proposal Data

For the pilot, we structured the offer not as a "bot subscription," but as a **Technical-Regulatory Diagnosis**:

1.  **Cost of Inaction:** A maximum fine under the new Data Protection Law is equivalent to about \$1.2 million USD.
2.  **Diagnosis Pricing:** Between 60 and 100 UF. Is it expensive? Not if you compare it to the 0.5% risk of a poorly managed audit.
3.  **Reimbursement Ready:** By integrating the flow with our **FONASA** engine, the system stops being a marketing expense and becomes a revenue enabler for telemedicine claims.

### What We Built: Compliance as Code

At **Examya**, the architectural decision was clear: compliance isn't a PDF you keep in a folder; it’s executable code.

We implemented a `ComplianceGuard` that audits every Shuri agent interaction. We don't allow the flow to proceed without a valid digital signature of the consent. This isn't a "feature" the patient sees; it’s the infrastructure protecting the clinic owner.

### The Builder's Gotcha: The Consultancy Trap

I learned something vital here: **Don't just be a lawyer who can't code, or a coder who doesn't know the law**.

Corporate clients seek "Operative Compliance." They don't want you to tell them what the law says (their lawyers already do that); they want you to give them the technical tool that **executes** the law without them having to touch a line of code.

### Why It Matters

2026 will be the year where "light health" startups will die under the weight of Chilean regulation. Those of us left standing will be the ones who understood that interoperability and privacy are the foundation of the building, not just the facade's paint.
