## The Illusion of Instant Quoting
It’s Saturday morning. You need to get a quote for some lab tests your doctor ordered. You take a quick photo of the prescription while walking, send it via WhatsApp, and... the bot tells you it can't read it. Frustrating, right?

## What Nobody Tells You About Medical OCR
Behind that WhatsApp response is an artificial intelligence engine (an OCR) trying to turn an image into useful text. Its job is not to guess: it is to recognize medical exams with enough confidence. Handwriting, stamps, shadows, or a cropped photo can make a task that looks simple much harder.

## The Real Data in Examya's Logs
This week at Examya, we reviewed real failures in the WhatsApp quoting flow. The `401` error came from an authentication mismatch: the route expected a user JWT, but the WhatsApp bridge was sending a service token. The Zod fallback came from a different mismatch: the prompt and the schema were not speaking exactly the same language.

A bad photo did not cause those production errors. But the review brought back a separate, everyday issue: even when the infrastructure is correct, a cropped, shadowed, or blurry prescription can prevent OCR from reading the ordered exams safely.

## Design Principle: Accuracy Over Speed
The right guardrail for this kind of flow is not to guess. If the reading does not reach sufficient confidence, the safe behavior should be to stop and ask for clarification or a clearer image before moving forward with a doubtful quote. In healthcare, clinical accuracy must come before blind speed.

## The Gotcha: It Is Not Just the Camera
You do not need a professional camera. What helps is more basic: good light, complete text, reasonable focus, and making sure the order is not cut off. A closer photo may help more than a distant one if it makes the full text readable, but there is no magic: if the document is out of frame, strongly shadowed, or too blurry, OCR can fail.

## Zoom Out: Educating the Patient Beats Forcing the AI
Helping the AI read your order can reduce friction: fewer calls, fewer trips to a lab just to ask for prices, and fewer back-and-forth messages because an image was not readable. It is a team effort: you send a legible photo; the system tries to do the heavy lifting more safely.

## What's Next
The next product guardrail is to separate problems more clearly: an infrastructure bug like the `401` is one thing, a misaligned contract like the Zod fallback is another, and a hard-to-read image is a different issue. The goal is to reduce friction without inventing certainty where there is none.
