# The trap of total automation: Why my AI agent has no "Publish" button

When you discover AI automation, the first temptation is to let go of the steering wheel entirely.

You give it access to the repository, set up a cron job on the VPS, and tell it: "Generate two weekly articles and push them to production."

It feels like magic, until you realize that artificial intelligence is brilliant at proposing, but terrible at judging the risk of its own actions.

Today I modified the pipeline of Cotocha, the orchestrator agent that helps me maintain this blog, to explicitly remove its permission to publish.

## The problem is not the LLM, it's the system design

In digital health—when we built the triage layer at Examya—we learned the hard way that a WhatsApp bot cannot give diagnoses or close a workflow without supervision. We needed a "human in the loop".

The problem is that at the infrastructure level, we usually think that this human should intervene at the very end, reviewing the production output. That is too late.

For my content ecosystem, I adapted the exact same architectural pattern from clinical health: **Strict separation between the generator, the review interface, and the compilation engine.**

## The Draft-First Pipeline

Instead of having one script do the entire cycle, I divided it into three isolated responsibilities:

**1. The Agent (The Scout):** Reads the context, reviews metrics, analyzes repositories, and generates the text. Its output is not a *commit*. Its output is writing a strict `.md` file in the `borradores/` (drafts) folder of my local Obsidian, along with social distribution suggestions.

**2. Obsidian (The Human Firewall):** The draft has no internet access. It is just a text file on my computer. There I read, edit, adjust the tone, and decide if the post adds value or is just algorithmic noise.

**3. The Executor (The local CI/CD):** Only after my visual approval in Obsidian, I run a second script (`blog-publish`). This is a deterministic process (no AI involved). It takes the approved `.md`, compiles it to `.mdx`, generates PDFs with Puppeteer for LinkedIn, verifies the frontmatter, and only then pushes to Vercel.

## The Zoom Out

Building truly useful agents is not about making them 100% autonomous. It's about designing the right "brakes".

In complex systems, intentional friction is a feature, not a bug. Forcing the system to stop at Obsidian and wait for my approval is what separates a professional publishing pipeline from an automated spam bot.

Total autonomy is for Twitter demos. In production, the agent proposes, but the human disposes.
