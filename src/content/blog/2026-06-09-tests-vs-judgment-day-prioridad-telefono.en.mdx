## 1. The Ghost in the Machine (Hook)

Yesterday we were running our adversarial review protocol, the famous **Judgment Day**, through Examya's `PurchaseHandler`. Everything was going well until one of the reviewer sub-agents, in a very confident tone, suggested: *"Hey, the 'from' field is the canonical origin of WhatsApp, it should have absolute priority over any other phone number."*

It sounded logical. If someone writes to you via WhatsApp, their number is the `from`, right? Well, no. We were about to approve the change when a unit test written months ago jumped at the throat: `PHONE-01: should prioritize registered patient phone over message source`. The "advanced" AI was wrong and the codebase was right.

## 2. The Vulnerability Nobody Tells You About (Lo que nadie cuenta)

In health bot development, the phone number is not just for chatting; it's an identity key. What nobody tells you is that the number from which a patient writes to you is not always the number they have registered in their clinical file or the one they want to use to receive their results.

If we prioritize the `from` (the WhatsApp account number) over the `patient_phone` (the explicit clinical data), we break traceability. A patient could be writing from their son's phone but needs the medical order to be associated with *their* official number. Blindly trusting the channel's metadata is the fastest way to dirty your database.

## 3. Cryptography in the First Millisecond (Datos reales)

To solve this in Examya, we established an immutable priority contract. It's not a suggestion; it's a law in our data model. The **PHONE-01**, **PHONE-02**, and **PHONE-03** tests document that the hierarchy must always be:

1.  **patient_phone**: The number the patient confirmed or that comes in the medical order.
2.  **phone**: The previously registered number in the profile.
3.  **from**: The physical number detected by the Meta webhook (the last resort).

## 4. The Gatekeeper (Lo que construiste)

We implemented this logic using a nullish coalescing chain that seems simple but is the anchor of our integrity. The technical part here is how we protect the `orderPayload` before it touches the database:

```typescript
// apps/agents/src/agents/shuri/handlers/purchase.handler.ts

async function handlePayment(context: ShuriContext, orderPayload: Partial<Order>) {
  // The sacred hierarchy that Judgment Day wanted to invert
  const finalPatientPhone = 
    orderPayload.patientPhone ?? // 1. Explicit order data
    context.patient?.phone ??    // 2. Registered profile data
    context.message.from;        // 3. WhatsApp channel fallback

  return await this.medicalSummaryService.generate({
    ...orderPayload,
    patientPhone: finalPatientPhone,
  });
}
```

## 5. The Gotcha: Why NestJS Can Work Against You (El gotcha)

Here is the real learning: AIs (even the best like Claude or Gemini) have a bias towards "cleanliness" and industry standards. Since WhatsApp says `from` is the origin, the AI assumes it's the source of truth.

But in complex systems with legacy business rules, the truth is not in the channel standard, but in the **test contract**. If we had followed the Judgment Day suggestion, we would have broken the flow for thousands of patients who use third-party numbers to manage their tests. The lesson: the AI proposes, but the test disposes.

## 6. More Than Code: It's Operational Compliance (Zoom out)

Why does this matter beyond a phone number? Because it proves that a robust test suite is not just for finding bugs; it is the **technical memory** of the project.

Without those PHONE-XX tests, we would have committed a subtle architectural error that we would have only detected weeks later in production, with patient complaints and crossed data. The "technical chore" of writing tests saved the afternoon yesterday.

## 7. What's Next: Context is King (Lo que viene)

This incident forced us to re-evaluate other metadata fields in PR #1004. We are applying the same "Documented Priority Hierarchy" logic to organization and medical center IDs. We are not going to let a "clean" suggestion from an AI erase months of field-tested business logic.
