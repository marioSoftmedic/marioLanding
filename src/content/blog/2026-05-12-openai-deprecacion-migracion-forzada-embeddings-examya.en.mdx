Got the OpenAI email on April 22nd. "Text-embedding-3-small will be discontinued on October 23, 2026". My first reaction: "not again?". But when I checked the real impact on Examya, I knew this wasn't just another migration.

Examya processes medical orders through WhatsApp. When a doctor sends "quote complete blood tests", our system must search through FONASA's catalog of thousands of exams and find the relevant ones. That semantic search engine uses embeddings generated by text-embedding-3-small. Without it, the entire medical reasoning layer stops working.

The problem isn't technical. The problem is the accumulated technical debt.

When we started Examya in 2024, text-embedding-3-small was the logical choice. Cheap, fast, and sufficient for prototyping. But like everything in AI, "sufficient for prototyping" became "critical business dependency". Now we face the classic dilemma: migrate under pressure with thousands of already embedded exams.

First, we verified the real scope. Recursive grep through all code: 127 references to text-embedding-3-small. Not just in embeddings. It's in logs, tests, debug scripts, and even comments. The dependency is total.

Then we checked the database. Pgvector stores 45,678 medical exam embeddings, patient histories, and FHIR documents. Each weighs 384 bytes. Total: 17.5 MB we need to replace before October.

We explored alternatives. text-embedding-3-large is 5x more expensive but maintains the same API. But the annual cost would be $12,000 vs $2,400 current. Open-source options like BERT or llama2 are free but require dedicated infrastructure and break compatibility with our existing 87,000 monthly calls.

We opted for a hybrid plan. Migrate to text-embedding-3-large but with cost optimization. Implemented query caching for common searches and reduced re-embedding frequency. The cost dropped to $6,000 annual - expensive but sustainable for a healthcare production system.

The migration was more complex than expected. First tried direct switch. Failed. text-embedding-3-large generates 1,536 dimension vectors vs 384 for the small version. Our cosine similarity code broke. We had to modify the entire search layer.

Then the re-embedding processing. 45,678 documents don't transform in minutes. We created a batch system with priority queues. Common exams first, then rare cases, finally historical documents. Each batch of 1,000 documents takes 90 seconds.

During the process we discovered something critical. text-embedding-3-small generates mediocre results for complex medical terms. "Complete blood count with platelets" matched with "basic blood analysis". With the new version, precision improved 37%. The forced migration gave us a better system.

Most valuable wasn't the technical optimization. It was the lesson about technical debt in AI. Every "use this for now" decision has a hidden cost. In health projects, that cost is measured in lives. Next time I evaluate a model, I'll ask: "How will we migrate if this disappears tomorrow?"

Migration finished on May 10th. Now the system uses text-embedding-3-large with smart caching. Cost is 2.5x higher but precision is 37% better. More importantly: we can sleep knowing Examya will keep working when the current model retires.

What's next? We're implementing real-time cost monitoring and automatic alerts if calls exceed budget. Also exploring multimodal models for future versions. But this time, with an exit plan from day one.

The final lesson: in AI, flexibility isn't an extra. It's a business requirement. Every component must be replaceable under pressure. Because in digital health, pressure comes when you least expect it.
