## The real problem with clinical evidence

You get an ARCO-P request from a patient: "I want my clinical data deleted". The DPO (Data Protection Officer) approves it, the deletion is executed, and a month later an audit arrives asking when, who, and why that information was deleted. You go look at the database... and the request record was also deleted. Or worse: the evidence is sitting in a rotated text log file in AWS that no one knows how to read.

## What nobody tells you about compliance

Law 21.719 doesn't just demand that you comply with ARCO-P rights (Access, Rectification, Cancellation, Opposition, Portability). It demands that you **prove** you complied on time and properly. A server log gets lost. A mutable JSON in the database has no validity because anyone with DB access can rewrite history. Compliance is not a visual feature; it's immutable forensic evidence.

## The hard data

During our technical analysis of the compliance regulations for Law 21.719 at Examya, we reached a design crossroads. Most web systems handle this kind of tracking by saving the history inside an `auditTrail` JSON field, nested within the request itself. But we realized there was a structural flaw: if an admin manually edits the JSON, or if the main ticket is accidentally deleted from the database by a cascading error, all the regulatory proof disappears with it.

## What we built: Append-Only Audit

We migrated to a purely *append-only* model in PostgreSQL. We built the `DsrAuditEventService` and a dedicated Prisma table where every state change, DPO approval, or SLA expiration is a new, immutable row. Zero `UPDATE`s, only `INSERT`s. 

And most importantly: we minimized the payload. The event stores the "what" and "who", but intentionally purges RUTs, clinical data, and raw bodies so the audit log itself doesn't become a sensitive data leak.

## The technical gotcha: ON DELETE RESTRICT

The most counterintuitive technical decision was the Foreign Key behavior. Typically, you'd use `ON DELETE CASCADE` so that if you delete a request, its history is deleted and your database stays clean. Here, we had to explicitly use `RESTRICT`. We **forbid** deleting the evidence from the database. 

Furthermore, we learned about failure tolerance: writing the audit event must fail "closed" (if I can't save the evidence, I abort the state change), but alert notifications must fail "open" (if I can't send the expired SLA email, the ticket must still be marked as expired).

## Zoom out: The impact on digital health

Healthcare software in Chile is facing a forced maturation. Legacy vendors are used to a simple console log being a sufficient excuse. When real audits start, systems that don't have their compliance modeled from the data layer are going to be a massive legal liability for the clinics and labs that hired them.

## What's next

Now that the evidence is immutable, the next step in Examya is the reporting engine. A dashboard where the institution's DPO can download the "Data Governance Report" with a single click and hand it to the auditor, featuring verifiable SLAs and immutable timestamps on every row.
