Digital health transformation in Chile usually centers on hospitals, large clinics, and telemedicine. But there's one actor that processes millions of medical orders per year and almost never shows up in the conversation.

Clinical laboratories.

## The problem nobody sees

Think about the typical flow. A patient leaves the doctor's office with a lab order. That order reaches the lab via fax, WhatsApp, or even in person on paper. The result comes back to the doctor when it can. Sometimes days later.

The process is manual, fragmented, and slow. And it directly impacts clinical decision-making.

At Examya we talk to labs every day. Small, medium, and large. The pattern repeats across almost all of them:

- Legacy systems that don't talk to each other
- Manual processes that consume hours of technical staff time
- Patients calling to ask about results that are already ready

## The lab as an information node

The interoperability proposed by Chile's Ley 21.668 isn't just a matter of technical standards. It's a real operational problem affecting labs right now.

And that's where the opportunity lies. The clinical lab isn't a passive link in the healthcare chain. It's an information node that, when properly digitized, can accelerate diagnoses, reduce errors, and improve the patient experience immediately.

Think about what labs already have: structured data from every test, patient history, normalized results. The information is there. The problem is how it flows.

## What we're building

At Examya we're connecting that node. Not from theory, but from daily conversations with those who process Chile's lab tests.

We've seen firsthand how a small lab can reduce order processing time by hours just with better integration. They don't need to replace all their systems. They need their existing systems to talk to each other.

The key isn't more technology. It's better connection.

## The real challenge

The true challenge of health interoperability isn't technical. It's about adoption. The standards exist (HL7, FHIR). The APIs exist. What's missing is for labs, especially small and medium ones across Chile, to have access to tools that connect their systems without requiring an in-house engineering team.

That's what we're solving.
