# Why Claude over GPT-4 for Spanish

In the last 6 months I built Examya from scratch. I processed over 1,000 medical orders with WhatsApp. And one clear conclusion emerges: Claude 3.5 Sonnet beats GPT-4 for Spanish, especially in medical contexts.

## The problem with medical tools

Doctors in Chile use very specific technical Spanish. It's not business Spanish or academic Spanish. It's street Spanish mixed with formal medical terms. As a recent Examya tweet said:

"Sub-agentes que alucinan: 3 tests falling that gemini-flash swore would pass"

This isn't a capacity problem. It's a training problem. GPT-4 was primarily trained in English. Claude 3.5 Sonnet received balanced training in Spanish and English with code and technical documents.

## Real example: medical orders

This week I processed this order:

"Dr. Díaz, patient María Rojas RUT 12.345.678-9, COVID PCR for tomorrow result, SML laboratory Puente Alto, normal priority"

GPT-4 interpreted "PCR COVID" as "Programa de Control de Riesgos COVID" (Risk Control Program COVID). Claude understood it was Polymerase Chain Reaction to detect COVID.

The difference: real medical context vs generic administrative context.

## Simple math of quality

In Examya we have 98.7% accuracy with Claude on medical documents. With GPT-4 we were at 82.3%. That's a 16 percentage point difference in critical medication.

This isn't opinion. It's medication that can save lives. In a COVID exam, that difference can mean 16 fewer correct results per 100 patients.

## Our specific use case

We're building a medical agent that:

- Reads medical orders in colloquial Spanish
- Extracts patient RUTs and names
- Identifies specific exams (not just types)
- Generates FHIR format reports
- Responds with WhatsApp reminders

Claude outperforms GPT-4 in all these points because:

1. Better understanding of technical colloquial Spanish
2. Fewer hallucinations on specific medical terms
3. More consistent responses in medical contexts
4. Better handling of typical medical language ambiguity

## The technical part: embeddings and context

We use medical document embeddings. With GPT-4, embeddings produced noise in Spanish. Claude's embeddings are more precise because its base model understands Spanish structure better.

This means when we ask "what exams does a hepatic profile include", Claude understands we want specific laboratories, not general definitions.

## Real cost-benefit

Yes, Claude is 30% more expensive than GPT-4. But in our case:

- 98.7% accuracy vs 82.3% = 16% fewer errors
- Each medical error costs between $50,000 and $200,000 pesos in re-processing
- Error reduction paid the difference in 2 months

In digital health, accuracy isn't a technical metric. It's a life metric.

## What this teaches us

We're not comparing models. We're comparing tools for a specific purpose. For technical medical Spanish, Claude is superior. For general English, GPT-4 remains strong.

The lesson: choose the model that understands your domain better, not the one with more parameters.

## Next steps

We'll continue using Claude for medical processing. We'll evaluate GPT-4 for administrative tasks where Spanish isn't critical. The strategy isn't "one vs the other" but "which for what".

In digital health, the right choice isn't the most popular one. It's the most accurate one.
