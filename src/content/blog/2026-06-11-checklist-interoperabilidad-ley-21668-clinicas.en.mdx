# A checklist to know if your clinic is ready for FHIR

A clinic can have online scheduling, a patient portal, WhatsApp, a laboratory system, and accounting software.

And still not be ready to interoperate.

That is the trap I keep seeing when we talk about digital health: we confuse having systems with having systems that understand each other.

Chile's Law 21.668 will push healthcare providers toward interoperability. But this problem is not solved by buying a new platform or promising to “connect everything”. That sounds good in a meeting, but in production it can break workflows that have kept the operation alive for years.

In healthcare, starting badly is expensive.

That is why in Fhirex, inside Examya, we are using a smaller and much more useful question:

> Can we represent one concrete clinical workflow in FHIR without touching production?

Not the whole medical record.

Not the whole LIS.

Not the whole network.

One workflow.

One order.

One specimen.

One result.

One auditable event.

## What nobody tells you about FHIR

FHIR is not magic.

FHIR does not fix broken processes.

FHIR does not automatically turn a PDF into reusable clinical data.

FHIR is a contract.

And like any contract, it rewards teams that understand what data exists, who creates it, when it is validated, and who needs to consume it later.

The hard part is not generating JSON shaped like a resource.

The hard part is answering operational questions that are often undocumented:

- Who validates the result?
- Where does the original order live?
- Is the patient identifier reliable?
- Which system owns the final truth?
- What evidence remains for an audit?

If those answers do not exist, FHIR only makes the mess look nicer.

## A simple checklist to start

Before buying, migrating, or building a FHIR server from scratch, I would review these five questions.

### 1. Do you have a bounded clinical workflow to test?

“Interoperate the clinic” is too big.

“Represent a lab order and its result” is manageable.

A good pilot starts with a small case:

```text
ServiceRequest → Specimen → Observation[] → DiagnosticReport
```

That workflow lets clinical, IT, and operations teams discuss something concrete.

Not an abstract architecture.

A patient, a specimen, and a result.

### 2. Can you test with synthetic data?

If the first step requires touching production, the project is already starting wrong.

The initial pilot should allow fake patients, simulated exams, and controlled results.

That lowers risk for IT, avoids unnecessary exposure of sensitive data, and lets the team test the contract before moving real information.

In Fhirex, that is a design principle: technical evidence first, real integration later.

### 3. Do you know which system is the source of truth?

In many clinics there is more than one “truth”.

The LIS has one piece of data.

The portal has another.

The operational spreadsheet has another.

And WhatsApp has the data the patient actually uses.

If you do not know which system owns each part of the workflow, interoperability becomes fragile.

FHIR needs ownership clarity.

Moving information is not enough. You need to know which data can be trusted.

### 4. Does your data have minimum traceability?

A clinical result without context is ambiguous.

To interoperate responsibly, the data should answer:

- who generated it;
- when it was generated;
- when it was validated;
- under which clinical order;
- which system transformed it;
- what evidence was recorded.

In FHIR, resources like `AuditEvent` and `Provenance` are not technical decoration.

They are the difference between “we have data” and “we can defend this data during an audit”.

### 5. Does the clinical team understand the benefit?

This point sounds soft, but it is not.

If interoperability is framed as “an IT project”, it will die in meetings.

The clinical team needs to understand the benefit in its own language:

- fewer calls about results;
- fewer patients carrying PDFs;
- fewer duplicate exams;
- less manual transcription;
- better continuity of care.

FHIR is technical.

But the pain it solves is deeply operational.

## The gotcha: do not start with the server

The typical mistake is starting with:

> Which FHIR server should we install?

I would start with a different question:

> Which clinical workflow can we represent safely, audibly, and without touching production?

The tool comes later.

First, define the contract.

What comes in.

What goes out.

What gets validated.

What gets traced.

Who reviews it.

That is the boring work, but it is what prevents a pilot from becoming a nice demo nobody can move to production.

## What we are building with Fhirex

Fhirex is not meant to replace existing clinical software.

It is meant to create a lateral layer that can represent current workflows in FHIR/Core-CL progressively.

The idea is simple:

- do not migrate everything;
- do not break the LIS;
- do not expose production at the beginning;
- use synthetic data;
- leave technical evidence that IT can review;
- move from one small workflow to real integrations.

In B2B healthcare, lowering adoption risk is more valuable than showing a spectacular screen.

## The zoom out

Interoperability in Chile will not be solved only by regulation.

The law pushes.

The standard helps.

But adoption happens when a clinic can say:

> “I can test this without risking my operation.”

That is the starting point.

Not connecting everything.

Not migrating everything.

Not buying out of fear.

Test a small, safe, auditable workflow.

Then scale.
