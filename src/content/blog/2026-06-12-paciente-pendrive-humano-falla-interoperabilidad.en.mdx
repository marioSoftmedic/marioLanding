# The patient as a "human thumb drive": The biggest failure of digital health

You go to the doctor. They give you a paper order for lab tests.

You walk to the lab. You hand over the paper. They draw your blood.

Three days later, you receive an email. You have to log into a portal, enter your ID and a temporary password, and download a PDF.

You go back to the specialist. You show them the PDF from your cracked phone screen or hand them a printed copy.

Congratulations. You are the healthcare system's API.

You have just become a "human thumb drive".

## What nobody tells you about the lack of integration

Millions of patients experience this scenario every day in LATAM.

And the underlying problem is that we believe that because the lab sends an email and has a web portal, we are doing "digital health".

It is not digital health. It is digitizing the mess.

Forcing the patient to be the physical or manual bridge between two systems (the clinic and the laboratory) is the rawest evidence that our clinical infrastructure has failed.

Legacy LIS and EHRs were designed to store data, not to share it.

The cost of this friction is brutal: duplicated exams, transcription errors, wasted clinical time, and a terrible patient experience.

## How we shut down the human API

At Examya and Fhirex, our thesis is that the data should move, not the patient.

A lab result should not be a "final document" handed to the patient to figure out what to do next. It should be a structured event in the healthcare network.

When we implement the interoperable layer with FHIR, the workflow changes:

1. The lab validates the exam.
2. Fhirex captures that validation, maps it against SNOMED CT / LOINC, and issues a `DiagnosticReport`.
3. That report travels directly via API (FHIR Subscription) to the treating physician's EHR.
4. The patient receives an automated WhatsApp message from Shuri saying: "Your results are ready and have already been sent to your doctor."

The patient goes from being the carrier of their own data to being the true beneficiary of the system.

## The Zoom Out

Law 21.668 in Chile is coming to force this.

It will no longer be optional for systems to talk to each other. Interoperating the clinical record will be mandatory.

But beyond the regulatory decree, clinics and labs must understand that keeping data in silos is no longer a competitive advantage. It is a commercial disadvantage.

Patients will prefer the network where their history flows on its own, without having to act as human thumb drives.
