Last Tuesday, while auditing a medical center's operations, I ran into the classic problem of Chilean healthcare. They have privacy policies beautifully drafted in a PDF and signed by management. However, in their day-to-day operations, patient results travel as unencrypted email attachments and move through legacy systems with no audit logs.

The Data Protection Officer (DPO) sleeps soundly believing the PDF protects them. The reality is they are operating blind.

## What nobody tells you

With Ley 21.719 (Data Protection Law), healthcare compliance stopped being a purely legal issue and became an infrastructure problem.

DPOs and CTOs assume risk is mitigated through contracts. What nobody mentions is that if a medical data leak occurs (the most sensitive data category that exists), the law demands auditable technical evidence. A contract doesn't trace a database log; interoperability does.

## Real data on the table

When we mapped operational leaks, we discovered that over 80% of sensitive information transit in independent labs happens outside regular channels (doctors' personal WhatsApps, lost printouts, forwarded emails). Without data standardization, building a manual Treatment Activity Register (RAT) is impossible.

## What we built: Fhirex

At Examya, we realized the solution for Ley 21.719 was leveraged by another regulation: Ley 21.668 (Health Interoperability).

That's why we built Fhirex. It operates as a technical-operational layer that standardizes transactions under the HL7 FHIR standard (specifically MINSAL's Core-CL guide). By forcing every exam, patient, and order to pass through this layer, we kill two birds with one stone.

## The architectural gotcha

The biggest technical challenge was solving passive traceability. At first, we tried mapping legacy systems by connecting directly to their databases, but the headache of dealing with someone else's technical debt is unmanageable.

The "gotcha" was intercepting data in transit. By requiring the clinical system or lab to communicate with Fhirex using `Patient` and `DiagnosticReport` FHIR profiles, RAT generation becomes automated.

```typescript
// Simplified interceptor in Fhirex
async function processDiagnosticReport(report: FHIRDiagnosticReport, ctx: RequestContext) {
  // 1. HL7 FHIR Standardization
  const validatedReport = await fhirValidator.validate(report, 'Core-CL');
  
  // 2. Automated RAT (Treatment Activity Register)
  await ratLogger.record({
    resourceType: 'DiagnosticReport',
    resourceId: validatedReport.id,
    accessedBy: ctx.requesterId,
    purposeOfUse: 'TREATMENT',
    timestamp: new Date()
  });
  
  return validatedReport;
}
```

Every interaction leaves an immutable footprint.

## Why it matters beyond the code

The Chilean digital health industry is maturing by force. MINSAL and CENS regulations aren't suggestions, they are barriers to entry. Institutions that view this as a legal expense will lose speed; those that understand it as an infrastructure upgrade will scale without risk.
