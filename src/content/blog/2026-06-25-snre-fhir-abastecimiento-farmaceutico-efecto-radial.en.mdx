When we talk about the new National Electronic Prescription System (SNRE) that the Ministry of Health made mandatory as of December 2025, the focus always falls on the same thing: the patient at the pharmacy counter.

But we are missing the big picture. The true impact of the SNRE, being based on the HL7 FHIR R4 standard, isn't in the last mile of sales. It's miles behind, in the supply chain of laboratories and the national pharmaceutical industry.

And if they don't see it coming, stockouts are going to hit harder than ever.

### The hidden angle: Traceability and Adherence

Today, a pharmaceutical lab produces medications based on historical estimates. They don't know exactly when a patient abandons a chronic treatment or at what exact moment a spike in prescriptions occurred in a specific region, until it's too late.

The adoption of FHIR R4 changes the rules of the game. By structuring clinical data, we move from "selling boxes" to having real traceability of dispensing and treatment adherence.

The conversation is no longer about integrating APIs. It's about technological infrastructure for the resilience of the healthcare system. If a chronic patient receives their reminders and management via WhatsApp (as we do with Examya), adherence goes up. If dispensing is reported in real-time via FHIR (as Fhirex does), the laboratory can adjust its logistics chain before a national stockout occurs.

### The Radial Effect of the Data Protection Law

Add to this a relentless accelerator: the new Data Protection Law (Ley 21.719).

Many believe the problem is an external hacker stealing data. The reality in healthcare centers is "Internal Risk": employees or vendors snooping through clinical records with no audit trail.

This is where what I call the **Radial Effect** happens. Large institutions (clinics, pharmacy chains, and pharma labs) are not going to assume the risk of million-dollar fines because of a small software vendor that doesn't comply. They will demand traceability of consent (ARCO-P rights) down their entire supply chain. Institutions will force small SaaS providers to comply with the law or simply cancel their contracts.

### Middleware as a shield

To survive this Radial Effect, building FHIR connectors "by hand" is no longer enough.

That's why we designed Fhirex as a RegTech middleware. It acts as a legal and technical shield for this secondary market. Small clinics and niche providers don't have to rebuild their systems from scratch; they connect to Fhirex, delegate FHIR routing to the SNRE, and inherit the access traceability required by the law.

### What's next

We are pivoting our B2B conversations. We are no longer just talking to IT managers about how to map JSON fields to FHIR. We are talking to the pharmaceutical industry about how structural interoperability protects their logistics chain and improves Real-World Data.

The SNRE is not a government web portal. It is the highway on which the operational survival of digital health in Chile will travel.
